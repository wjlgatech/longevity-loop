# Longevity / Aging Biology — The 30-Year Survival Window (1996–2026)

🕰 last30years v1.0.0 · window 1996–2026 · 6 survivors ranked

**Method.** Ranked strictly by *survival evidence* — still-used, built-upon,
survived-the-hype — never by launch buzz (LAW 1). Each item runs three probes:
**P1 still-used** (production/curriculum/standard today), **P2 built-upon**
(named descendants that assume it), **P3 hype-survived** (declared dead/limited
at least once and persisted). Tiers: `🏛 survived` (3/3, cited) ·
`🌳 aging-well` (2/3) · `⚪ unproven` (lead, not finding). Born-year and survival
age are stated for every item; anything born <10 years ago is capped at `⚪` on a
30-year question (LAW 6). A mandatory graveyard (Anti-Portfolio) follows.

---

## Survived — Ranked Survival Clusters

### 1. The Hallmarks of Aging framework — born 2013, age 13 🏛
The organizing grammar of the whole field. López-Otín, Blasco, Partridge,
Serrano & Kroemer's 2013 *Cell* paper defined nine hallmarks (genomic
instability, telomere attrition, epigenetic alterations, loss of proteostasis,
deregulated nutrient-sensing, mitochondrial dysfunction, cellular senescence,
stem-cell exhaustion, altered intercellular communication).
- **P1 still-used:** it remains the default scaffold taught and cited across
  aging biology; the 2013 paper is one of the most-cited papers in all of
  biology (12,000+ per single index, tens of thousands overall).
- **P2 built-upon:** the authors' own 2023 *Cell* update expanded it to twelve
  hallmarks (adding compromised autophagy, dysbiosis, chronic inflammation),
  and reviews now use "hallmarks" as the conceptual frame for intervention
  design.
- **P3 hype-survived:** criticized as a descriptive/arbitrary list rather than a
  causal theory, and challenged by the "expand vs. consolidate" debate — yet it
  survived by being revised, not discarded.
- **Lindy read:** 13 years of accelerating use through one major revision ⇒ the
  frame outlives any single hallmark; expect it to remain the field's shared
  vocabulary well past 2030.

### 2. Epigenetic (DNA-methylation) clocks — born 2013, age 13 🏛
Horvath's pan-tissue clock and Hannum's blood clock (both 2013) turned
"biological age" from metaphor into a measurable number (Horvath: 353 CpGs,
r≈0.96 with chronological age).
- **P1 still-used:** actively used in 2024–2025 studies and commercial
  biological-age tests; Horvath/Hannum remain reference clocks.
- **P2 built-upon:** an entire second/third generation *assumes* them —
  DNAm PhenoAge (Levine 2018), GrimAge, and DunedinPACE (pace-of-aging) —
  built to predict morbidity/mortality rather than chronological age.
- **P3 hype-survived:** hit hard by the "correlational not causal," "trained and
  tested on the same public repositories," and "do we actually need aging
  clocks?" critiques — and survived as the dominant aging biomarker anyway.
- **Lindy read:** the *measurement paradigm* is durable even as individual
  clocks churn; the open question (see Gaps) is causal validation.

### 3. mTOR / rapamycin geroscience — born ~2009, age 17 🏛
The most robust pharmacological lifespan result in mammals. The NIA
Interventions Testing Program (ITP) showed rapamycin extends lifespan in
genetically heterogeneous mice of both sexes (median +23% male, +26% female at
42 ppm) — notably even when started late in life.
- **P1 still-used:** rapamycin/rapalogs are the benchmark geroprotector in
  ongoing preclinical and early human "geroscience" trials.
- **P2 built-upon:** it anchored the *geroscience hypothesis* (target aging to
  delay multiple diseases at once) and the nutrient-sensing hallmark; every new
  candidate is implicitly compared to it.
- **P3 hype-survived:** persistent skepticism over immunosuppression, dosing,
  and clinical translation — "current animal data do not support rapamycin as a
  reliable off-the-shelf intervention" — yet it remains the field's positive
  control that refuses to die.
- **Lindy read:** 17 years and multi-species replication ⇒ the mechanism (mTOR
  inhibition) is load-bearing regardless of whether rapamycin itself becomes a
  human longevity drug.

### 4. Single-cell methods + aging cell atlases — born ~2009–2015, age ~11–17 🏛
scRNA-seq (Tang 2009; Drop-seq/10x Genomics ~2015) became the microscope of
molecular aging. Tabula Muris Senis (2020) profiled 300,000+ cells across 23
mouse tissues at multiple ages.
- **P1 still-used:** single-cell/single-nucleus profiling is now standard
  methodology across aging studies and consortia.
- **P2 built-upon:** cell atlases, spatial transcriptomics, single-cell
  biological-age models, and the training corpora for biology foundation models
  all assume it.
- **P3 hype-survived:** early "expensive, noisy, batch-effect-ridden" skepticism
  gave way to it becoming infrastructure.
- **Lindy read:** durable as a *platform*; the science built on top will keep
  changing, the method won't disappear.

### 5. Senolytics / cellular senescence as a druggable target — born 2015, age 11 🌳
Zhu, Kirkland et al. (2015) identified the first senolytics (dasatinib +
quercetin, D+Q) that selectively kill senescent cells.
- **P1 still-used:** D+Q and navitoclax are in active human pilot trials through
  2024–2026 (diabetic kidney disease, mild cognitive impairment, osteoarthritis,
  psychiatric aging).
- **P2 built-upon:** spawned the NIH SenNet consortium and the Senotherapeutics
  Biomarker Consortium (2025); senescence is a named hallmark.
- **P3 hype-survived (partial):** no senolytic is FDA-approved and several
  endpoints remain modest — but the field persists and institutionalized rather
  than collapsing. Missing the clean "approved-drug" survival proof keeps this
  at 🌳, not 🏛.
- **Lindy read:** the *target* is durable; whether first-gen senolytics
  translate is still open.

### 6. Reconciled caloric restriction primate science — born (studies) 1987/1989, reconciled 2017, age ~9 (reconciliation) 🌳
The UW–Madison (benefit) vs NIA (no significant survival benefit) rhesus-monkey
CR studies famously contradicted each other (2009 vs 2012), then the two rival
teams jointly reconciled the discrepancy in *Nature Communications* (2017),
attributing it largely to control-diet and study-design differences.
- **P1 still-used:** CR remains the reference dietary intervention and the origin
  of the nutrient-sensing program.
- **P2 built-upon:** motivated CR-mimetics (rapamycin, metformin) and the
  human CALERIE trials.
- **P3 hype-survived:** the simple "CR extends primate lifespan" headline did
  *not* cleanly survive; the durable survivor is the more careful, reconciled
  conclusion (robust healthspan benefit, context-dependent lifespan effect).
- **Lindy read:** a case study in how honest reconciliation, not the original
  hype, is what actually persists.

### Emerging — real but too young to rank (LAW 6)
- **AlphaFold2 (born 2020, age 6):** near-experimental protein-structure
  prediction (CASP14), 200M+ structures released, adopted as standard tooling by
  1M+ researchers, Lasker award. Extraordinary adoption, but at 6 years it
  cannot be scored above emerging on a 30-year question. Strongest candidate to
  become a `🏛` survivor by 2030.
- **Single-cell foundation models — scGPT, Geneformer (born 2023, age 3):** see
  `⚪ unproven`; genuinely contested whether they beat simple baselines.

## Did-Not-Survive / Stalled — The Anti-Portfolio
*(louder at birth than several survivors above; dead, refuted, or stuck — LAW 4)*

- **Resveratrol & sirtuin (SIRT1) activators / Sirtris — born ~2003, peak 2008,
  dead by 2013.** GSK bought Sirtris for ~$720M (2008) on resveratrol-activates-
  SIRT1 lifespan claims. Pfizer (2010) and Amgen showed the SIRT1 activation was
  an *assay artifact* (only worked with a fluorophore-tagged substrate); the
  SRT501 clinical program was halted after renal failures in a myeloma trial;
  GSK closed the Sirtris labs in 2013. The single loudest longevity bet of the
  2000s.
- **Antioxidant supplements as anti-aging (the interventional free-radical
  theory) — hyped 1990s–2000s, refuted 2000s–2010s.** Large randomized-trial
  meta-analyses (Bjelakovic et al.) found antioxidant supplements do not extend
  life and some (beta-carotene, vitamin E, high-dose) *increase* mortality. The
  theory as an intervention did not survive; oxidative-stress biology persists in
  more nuanced form.
- **"Young blood" plasma transfusion / Ambrosia — hyped 2016–2018, shut down
  2019.** Ambrosia sold young-donor plasma at ~$8,000/L with no controlled
  efficacy data; the FDA warned in Feb 2019 that such infusions have "no proven
  clinical benefit" and carry real risks; Ambrosia halted the same day.
  (Legitimate parabiosis-factor research continues — but the commercialized
  product died.)
- **NAD⁺ boosters (NMN, NR) as longevity drugs — hyped 2016–2021, stalled.**
  Reliably raise NAD⁺ in humans, but human functional/longevity benefits are
  "modest or inconsistent"; no proven anti-aging effect in humans despite
  invertebrate lifespan claims. A textbook preclinical-to-human translation
  gap, not (yet) a survivor.
- **The simple monkey-CR lifespan claim** (see reconciled entry #6) — the
  headline did not survive; only the qualified conclusion did.

## ⚪ Unproven this run (leads, not findings)
- **Partial (Yamanaka-factor) reprogramming / cellular rejuvenation — born 2016
  (Ocampo), age 10.** Scientifically alive and enormously funded (Altos Labs
  ~$3B, 2022), with a 2024 peer-reviewed wild-type mouse lifespan result
  (Macip et al.) — but almost entirely preclinical, no clinical validation, and
  the tumorigenesis/delivery risks are unresolved. Treat as a high-variance
  lead, not a survivor.
- **Single-cell foundation models (scGPT, Geneformer), born 2023.** Rapid
  uptake, but 2025 analyses argue their attention "captures co-expression rather
  than unique regulatory signal," and interpretability/benchmark claims are
  contested. Too young and too unsettled to score.
- **Metformin as a geroprotector (TAME framing, ~2015).** The TAME trial (Nir
  Barzilai/AFAR, ~3,000 adults) is the field's attempt at a regulatory
  precedent, but as of 2024–2025 it remains only partially funded and not
  reporting; the durable claim is still unproven.

## Gaps (survival window)
*Durable unsolved problems, and tools/benchmarks/standards that SHOULD exist
given what survived but still don't.*

- **Shared cross-study benchmarks for aging biomarkers.** Clocks are routinely
  trained *and* tested on the same public methylation repositories, inviting
  publication bias; there is no common held-out benchmark or leaderboard with
  standardized tasks (mortality, multimorbidity, intervention response). The
  Biomarkers of Aging Consortium (NIA symposia 2023–2024) is the seed, but no
  accepted standard yet exists.
- **Causal vs. correlational clock validation.** The single biggest survivor
  (epigenetic clocks) still cannot distinguish drivers from passengers of aging;
  Mendelian-randomization "causal clocks" so far do *not* outperform
  second-generation correlational clocks. A validated causal-inference framework
  for aging biomarkers is missing.
- **A standardized, universal senescent-cell definition / marker.** SenNet
  (2024) could only issue *tissue-specific* detection recommendations because no
  single universal marker exists — which blocks clean, comparable endpoints for
  senolytic trials. A qualified biomarker panel is needed and not yet ratified.
- **Reproducibility / replication infrastructure beyond mice.** The NIA ITP is a
  gold-standard, blinded, multi-site replication engine *for mouse lifespan* —
  there is no equivalent standardized replication pipeline for human trials,
  cell-based senescence assays, or reprogramming claims, where irreproducibility
  has repeatedly burned the field (sirtuins, young blood).
- **Regulator-qualified surrogate endpoints for human longevity trials.**
  "Aging" is not an approvable indication and no biomarker is FDA-qualified as a
  surrogate; TAME is stuck partly on this. Without validated surrogates, human
  geroscience trials stay slow, huge, and underfunded.
- **FAIR data + metadata standards for multi-omic aging data.** Single-cell and
  multi-omic aging datasets are fragmented across incompatible formats/metadata,
  limiting the very foundation-model training and cross-study meta-analysis the
  field now wants. A shared aging-data standard and interoperable repository is
  overdue.
- **A standardized preclinical-to-human translation gate.** Every major
  graveyard item (resveratrol, NAD boosters, young blood, antioxidants) died in
  the mouse→human jump. There is no agreed go/no-go translational protocol
  (dose scaling, biomarker responsiveness, pre-registration) to catch these
  before commercialization.
- **Aging-specific benchmarks for biology foundation models.** As scFMs and
  AlphaFold-class models enter aging research, there is no aging-relevant
  benchmark suite proving they beat simple baselines on aging tasks (biological
  age, senescence classification, intervention prediction) — leaving adoption
  ahead of validation.
