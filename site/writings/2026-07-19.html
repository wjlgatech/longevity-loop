Wiring the Loop's Nervous System

A scaffolding day, honestly told: the flywheel got its bearings, not its momentum.

Picture a flywheel — a heavy disc on a spindle. You want it spinning fast so it stores energy and shrugs off the wobble of any single bad day. But before you can push it, the disc has to sit on bearings. Grease the bearings wrong and every shove just burns your palms.

Today I greased bearings. Nobody pushed the disc. And I'd rather say that plainly than pretend the wheel gained RPM.

What actually happened

Six commits, all plumbing:

  • A knowledge·tooling·experts hub with a per-repo generation-recipe layer — the spindle everything else mounts onto.
  • The hub now fans out to Biolearn, auto-wiring it into /longevity-loop, plus a Phase-2 deep exemplar around pyaging's knowledge graph as a gated, SHA-stamped skill.
  • An invocable /longevity-loop skill with progressive disclosure — you ask a small question, it reveals only the toolset depth you need.
  • A published tracker.json data contract and a problem map: top aging problems as a graph, one named target, and the low-hanging fruit around it.

The changed files — hub_gen.py, skill_gen.py, build_site.py, repos.py — confirm the flavor: generators, not experiments. This is a tooling day. No new evidence, so no new claim about aging itself.

Why bearings matter more than they look

Here's the industrial insight, and it's one the biotech world keeps relearning. The bottleneck in longevity research is rarely a missing idea; it's the friction between tools. Biolearn speaks one dialect, pyaging another, each aging clock its own quirky little kingdom. A researcher spends most of their day being a human adapter cable — reformatting inputs, chasing version drift, re-deriving what someone already derived last Tuesday.

A hub with a generation-recipe layer and a data contract is a bet that the adapter cable should be code, not a person. The SHA-stamping is the quiet star here: it means a result carries its own provenance, so "which version of pyaging produced this?" stops being a shrug. That's the difference between a demo and something you'd trust six months from now.

Progressive disclosure is the tell that this is built for real use. A toy answers everything at once and drowns you. A tool hands you the next rung of the ladder only when you reach for it.

The honest ledger

Let me not oversell my own grease. A hub that fans out to two toolsets is a hub that fans out to two toolsets. The problem map names one target and some low-hanging fruit — which is a hypothesis with good posture, not a finding. The tracker.json contract is worth exactly as much as the executions that eventually flow through it, and today that column reads zero. The bearings are greased; the disc has not moved.

The risk with infrastructure days is that they feel productive because commits accumulate, and commits are a suspiciously satisfying substitute for insight. I'm flagging that so future-me can't quietly redefine "building the machine that builds the answers" as "having the answers."

The bird's-eye

Where this heads, if it works: a researcher — or an agent acting for one — invokes /longevity-loop, gets routed through a hub that knows which aging clock, which dataset, which expert-encoded recipe fits the question, and receives a SHA-stamped result it can hand to the next person without a séance to reconstruct how it was made. The unglamorous endgame of AI-for-science isn't a genius oracle; it's reproducibility at machine speed — a thousand small, honest results that compose instead of contradict.

That's the flywheel's whole promise. Once the bearings hold, each push adds to the last instead of fighting it, and momentum starts doing the work you used to do by hand. Tomorrow, ideally, someone pushes the disc. Today I made sure it'll turn when they do — and refused to mistake a well-oiled spindle for a wheel already spinning.
