Building the Flywheel Before Spinning It

A day of bolting parts onto the machine: a frontier tracker, a synthesizer, and an execution ledger. No claims today — just the apparatus that will one day make claims cheap.

Look at the flywheel above. A flywheel is a heavy wheel that's miserable to get moving and then wonderful once it is — every push adds momentum you don't have to pay for again. That's the whole thesis of this project: build a research loop where each turn stores energy for the next.

Today I didn't push the wheel. I bolted the parts on. And that's fine — a flywheel with a loose axle just wobbles.

What actually shipped

The commit log is honest about it. This was an infrastructure day:

  • A Frontier Radar that tracks recent aging works, made robust against arXiv's 429 rate-limiting by leaning on GitHub as the primary source. (Nothing humbles ambition like an HTTP 429.)
  • A synthesizer that reads the frontier and drafts roadmap proposals — each one backed by a real Europe PMC record, none of them promoted to a claim.
  • An execution ledger: checkbox plus a before→after eval per execution, gated.
  • Turn-01 anchored to Levine's Systems Age as the highest-signal starting baseline.
  • A recap pipeline that — after a fix to dispatch pages.yml — actually deploys the thing you're reading.

So the wheel now has a hub (the tracker), spokes (the synthesizer), and a brake (the human-review gate). No spin yet.

The one design decision worth defending

The synthesizer ran and found genuinely newer reviews than my curated entries — a 2026 Nature Medicine review on biological aging clocks matched to Wyss-Coray, a Gladyshev-matched review on epigenetic dysregulation, a Barzilai-matched study on 'super movers' over 80. Tempting to just ingest them.

I didn't. Every one of those is stamped DRAFT — confirm identity first. The tool matched on Wyss-Coray T and Gladyshev V — initials, not proof. A same-surname collision would quietly poison the frontier with someone else's paper.

NO EVIDENCE ⇒ NO CLAIM. A proposal backed by a real record is still just a proposal until a human opens the link and checks it's the same person.

This is the unglamorous core of the brand. The machine is allowed to suggest. It is never allowed to assert. The quote field in the schema demands a verbatim sentence pasted from the abstract — no paraphrase, because paraphrase is where hallucination sneaks in wearing a lab coat.

The industrial insight

Here's what I keep noticing: in solo research the bottleneck is almost never ideas. It's the cost of verifying whether an idea is still true. Papers get superseded, authors get confused with namesakes, your six-month-old curated list quietly rots. Most people solve this by not checking. The synthesizer is my attempt to make checking routine and cheap — it does the boring 90% (find the newer paper, fetch the abstract, flag the identity risk) and hands me the irreducible 10% (is this the right human, is this the right sentence).

That division of labor — machine proposes, human ratifies — is going to be the shape of most serious AI-assisted research. Not autonomous agents making discoveries. Amanuenses that make the frontier legible so a human can spend judgment where judgment matters.

Bird's-eye: where this points

The roadmap now has an Act II: a sequenced wet-lab bridge and, eventually, a company spin-out. Today's plumbing is what makes that credible rather than delusional. If the loop can reliably (1) watch the frontier, (2) draft testable moves, and (3) log honest before→after evals, then the jump from in-silico baseline to a real wet-lab hypothesis becomes an engineering path instead of a leap of faith.

The flywheel isn't turning yet. But it has an axle that won't wobble, a brake that won't let it lie, and Levine's Systems Age loaded as the first weight. Tomorrow, we push.
