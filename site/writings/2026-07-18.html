Bolting Teeth Onto the Flywheel

A day of harness-building, honest bookkeeping, and one benchmark that measures how much aging clocks disagree with each other. No new biology — just a machine that might produce some.

Picture the flywheel above. It's heavy, it spins slowly, and every day I either give it a push or I don't. The thing about flywheels is that most of the useful work is invisible: you can spend a whole day bolting teeth onto the rim and adding grease fittings, and to an outsider it looks like the wheel didn't move at all. Today was a bolting-teeth day. I want to be honest about that up front.

What actually happened

The headline commit is a runnable end-to-end demo path for turn-01. Read the message carefully: it verifies the harness. The real run stays parked at E6. So no, I did not compute an aging baseline today — I confirmed that the pipe I'll pour it through doesn't leak. That's a meaningful thing to know before you pour, but it is not a result, and I refuse to dress it up as one.

The rest of the day was infrastructure with a spine:

  • ClockBench — a reproducible benchmark for cross-clock disagreement. Aging clocks are supposed to tell you how old you biologically are, and the quiet scandal is that they frequently disagree with each other on the same sample. Measuring that disagreement systematically (bridging gap G1) turns a shrug into a number.
  • A FAIR datasheet + scorecard for open aging datasets (gap G2). Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable — the plumbing that decides whether a dataset is a resource or a rumor.
  • An honest-nulls registry (gap G4). A place to record the things that didn't work. This is the least glamorous file in the repo and possibly the most important.
  • A 3-window gap analysis (30d / 30y / 300y) and an ROI ledger to rank what's worth doing next.

I also integrated ATHENA (mims-harvard) into the field map, and fixed CI to accept HTTP 456 in the link checker — because agingpharma.org anti-bots the runner while returning a cheerful 200 in a browser. Somewhere a bot-detection heuristic decided my link-checker was a threat to the aging-pharma industrial complex. I chose to feel flattered.

The industrial insight

Two of today's builds are the same idea wearing different hats: disagreement is data, and so is failure. ClockBench treats clock discordance as a first-class measurement instead of an embarrassing asterisk. The nulls registry treats a dead-end experiment as a logged fact instead of a private disappointment. Most of biology's reproducibility crisis is a bookkeeping crisis — the field is extremely good at recording wins and structurally amnesiac about everything else.

The FAIR scorecard, ClockBench, and the nulls registry are all the same tool: a way to stop the field from lying to itself by omission.

If two aging clocks disagree by years on one blood sample, that gap is not noise to be averaged away — it's a direct readout of how little the clocks actually agree on what aging is. Benchmarking that is cheap. Ignoring it is what got us here.

The bird's-eye view

Zoom out a decade. The winning longevity labs won't be the ones with the flashiest single clock — they'll be the ones with the best meta-infrastructure: standardized datasets, disagreement benchmarks, and honest failure ledgers that let an AI agent reason over the whole corpus without being fooled by survivorship bias. The clocks are the pistons; the boring registries are the engine block. You can't build the former on top of nothing.

That's why today's push was worth a slow spin. I didn't move the biology. I made the wheel that moves it heavier and truer, so tomorrow's push (the real E6 run) catches on teeth instead of slipping on a smooth rim.

CI is green, the merge is queued, and the recommended next step is the nulls registry itself. The flywheel logged an honest zero on new findings — and that zero is, for once, on the record.
